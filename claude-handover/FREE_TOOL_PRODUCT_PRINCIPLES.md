# KKME.eu — Free Tool Product Principles
## Purpose, utility, and what would make it genuinely valuable

---

## 1. What KKME is for

KKME exists to reduce uncertainty in Baltic BESS decision-making.

It should help serious users answer a small number of high-value questions faster and more honestly than they could using:
- scattered TSO data
- consultant decks
- sponsor models
- industry gossip
- internal spreadsheets

KKME is not:
- a broker site
- a sales funnel
- a generic energy dashboard
- a lender-grade underwriting tool
- a trading terminal

KKME is:
- a Baltic BESS market intelligence layer
- a reference-asset economics screen
- a project-pipeline reality check
- a structural timing / competition / buildability view

---

## 2. The real user value

The site is useful only if it helps users make better strategic decisions.

### Developers
Need:
- whether timing is getting worse
- whether the market is getting crowded
- whether project assumptions still make sense
- whether a planned COD is becoming commercially weak

### Investors
Need:
- a sanity check on sponsor assumptions
- an external reference asset
- a clearer sense of Baltic competitive pressure
- project-pipeline realism, not just sponsor optimism

### Suppliers / EPC / optimizers / counterparties
Need:
- where real activity is happening
- which projects are progressing
- whether the market is still opening or already compressing
- who is real and who is still speculative

KKME should help them think more clearly, not persuade them toward one answer.

---

## 3. Product principles

### A. Utility over decoration
Every section should answer a real question.
If a component does not improve a decision, remove it.

### B. Honest uncertainty
Do not pretend every number is equally true.
Always distinguish:
- observed
- proxy
- derived
- modeled

### C. Baltic specificity is the edge
Do not dilute the product into generic European market wallpaper.
Europe-wide references are context only.
Baltic specificity is the reason KKME matters.

### D. Fewer stronger views
A small number of high-value synthesized views is better than many weak signals.

### E. No fake precision
Do not imply confidence beyond what the source quality supports.
Especially:
- project stages
- COD timing
- balancing prices
- Revenue Engine assumptions

### F. Structural neutrality
BESS is not "good" or "bad."
The market is what it is.
The tool should describe conditions, pressure, and implications without trying to sell optimism or pessimism.

### G. Trust compounds slowly
One wrong project stage, one stale number, or one obviously mislabeled metric does more damage than five nice features create.

---

## 4. What would make KKME genuinely great

### 1. Baltic project truth layer
The single most valuable thing is a disciplined Baltic project universe.

This means:
- projects are real
- duplicates are resolved
- stages are meaningful
- CODs are confidence-scored
- operational vs commissioning vs market-active are separated
- every important record has traceability

This matters more than most UI improvements.

### 2. Reference asset honesty
The Revenue Engine does not need to be perfect.
It needs to be honest.

That means:
- assumptions are visible
- source classes are visible
- modeled output is not presented as market truth
- the tool explains movement, not just output
- metrics are labeled correctly
- sensitivity is visible without turning into a toy calculator

### 3. Structural market compression views
The most useful insight is often not price alone, but how timing and competition are changing the opportunity.

The product is strongest when it makes these things visible:
- fleet growth
- S/D compression
- buildability pressure
- queue / permit saturation
- duration trade-offs
- timing deterioration

### 4. Confidence-aware intelligence
Not all information should be treated equally.
A good free tool should show not just numbers, but confidence in those numbers.

Examples:
- project status confidence
- permit confidence
- operational fleet confidence
- revenue-input source quality
- data freshness and fallback state

### 5. Recheck value
The site should reward repeat visits.

Users should come back because:
- pipeline changed
- timing changed
- balancing conditions shifted
- a new project became real
- a market assumption moved
- the reference asset moved meaningfully

---

## 5. What to improve next — high-value priorities

### Priority 1 — Data truth and confidence
Build a formal source hierarchy and confidence system across:
- projects
- S2 fleet
- S4 buildability
- Revenue Engine inputs

### Priority 2 — Fleet intelligence quality
Improve:
- project stage classification
- COD confidence
- operational vs market-active distinction
- public evidence vs manual claims separation
- source traceability

### Priority 3 — Revenue Engine usefulness
Keep refining:
- truthful metric labels
- scenario movement explanation
- compact sensitivity view
- assumption transparency
- no fake certainty

### Priority 4 — Buildability realism
Strengthen:
- permit parsing
- reservation pressure
- queue signals
- Lithuania-specific public grid constraints
- clear distinction between headline headroom and practical access

### Priority 5 — External benchmark calibration
Maintain an external benchmark layer for:
- CAPEX ranges
- O&M ranges
- financing context
- European contract / auction clears
- public return references

Use benchmarks for calibration and credibility, not as automatic truth.

---

## 6. What to avoid

### A. Generic energy content
Do not add broad "energy transition" content that does not sharpen BESS decisions.

### B. Too many charts
One more chart is not automatically one more insight.

### C. Sales framing
Do not turn neutral observations into recommendations.

### D. Benchmark clutter
European context is useful only if it helps interpret the Baltics.

### E. Hidden fallback logic
If data falls back from observed to proxy, that should be knowable.

### F. Overloading the page
The site should feel composed, not like every useful thing has been added at once.

---

## 7. The minimum product promise

> The fastest honest way to understand whether a Baltic BESS project story still makes sense under current competition, timing, buildability, and market structure.

If the site does that well, it is valuable even if it stays free.

---

## 8. Suggested internal checklist for future features

Before adding any new section, ask:

1. What real decision does this improve?
2. Is the underlying data trustworthy enough?
3. Is this Baltic-specific or just generic context?
4. Does it reduce uncertainty, or just add information?
5. Will a serious user come back for it?
6. Can we explain the source quality honestly?
7. Does it fit the current product hierarchy, or create clutter?

If the answer is weak, do not add it.

---

## 9. Best long-term outcome

The best outcome is not "lots of traffic."
It is that a small number of serious market participants quietly start relying on KKME because it is:
- specific
- current
- honest
- useful
- difficult to replicate without doing the actual work

That is enough.
